The Science Behind the Ratings

How we measure skill, uncertainty, and fairness

TrueSkill — A Bayesian Rating System

We use TrueSkill, a Bayesian skill rating system developed by Microsoft Research in 2006. Unlike Elo (designed for 1v1 chess), TrueSkill was built specifically for multiplayer team games — exactly the kind of team-based matches we play at our LAN parties.

The core idea: every player is described by two numbers, not just one.

  • μ (mu) — the system's best estimate of your true skill level. Think of it as "how good we think you are."
  • σ (sigma) — the uncertainty around that estimate. Think of it as "how sure we are." A new player has high σ (we know little); a veteran has low σ (we're confident).

After each game, TrueSkill compares what should have happened (based on team ratings) with what actually happened. If the underdog wins, the update is large. If the favorite wins as expected, the update is small. Both μ and σ adjust — your rating moves and the system becomes more or less certain about you.

Our Parameter Choices

TrueSkill has several tuning knobs. Here's what we chose and why:

  • Initial μ = 25.0 (displayed as 1000) — Every new player starts here. We multiply by a scaling factor of 40 to get familiar Elo-like numbers (25 × 40 = 1000).
  • Initial σ = 8.33 (μ ÷ 3) — The default TrueSkill convention. This means we're initially saying "this player's true skill is probably somewhere between ~670 and ~1330" (within ±1 σ scaled).
  • β = 4.17 (σ ÷ 2) — The performance variance. This models the fact that even a player of known skill won't perform identically every game. Higher β means more randomness in outcomes; ours is moderate, reflecting that AoE2 has significant game-to-game variance (map generation, civilization matchups, strategy choices).
  • τ = 0.167 (σ ÷ 50) — The dynamic factor. After each game, σ ticks up slightly by τ before the update. This prevents ratings from "locking in" too early — it acknowledges that players improve (or get rusty) over time. Our small τ means ratings are responsive but stable.
  • Draw probability = 0.0 — AoE2 doesn't have draws. One team wins, one team loses.

Confidence Score

The "Confidence" percentage you see on the leaderboard tells you how much the system has learned about a player:

Confidence = (1 − σcurrent ÷ σinitial) × 100%

A brand-new player is at 0%. As they play more games, σ shrinks and confidence climbs. A player with 80%+ confidence has a very stable rating. Players below a threshold are listed as "Unproven Warriors" on the leaderboard — their ratings are still settling.

Rating Re-centering

There's a subtle problem with TrueSkill: it's not zero-sum. In Elo, if you gain 20 points your opponent loses 20. In TrueSkill, the total μ across all players tends to drift downward over time. This happens because:

  • Asymmetric sigma effects — when uncertainty decreases, it can pull the average μ down slightly.
  • New player entry — new players start at 1000 but quickly lose rating in their first games while they find their true level, dragging the overall average down.

Left uncorrected, after hundreds of games the group average might sit at 950 or lower, making everyone's rating look deflated.

Our fix: we compute a cosmetic offset so the group average is always displayed as exactly 1000:

offset = 1000 − average(μscaled)

This offset is applied to every displayed rating — leaderboard, charts, team generator, player profiles. But it never touches the underlying data. The raw μ and σ values in the database and in TrueSkill calculations are untouched. Per-game rating deltas (Δ) are also unaffected, since the constant offset cancels out when computing differences.

Handicap Processing

Age of Empires II: Definitive Edition has a built-in handicap system that boosts a player's economy and production speed. It ranges from 100% (normal) to 200% (double economy), in 5% increments.

We extract each player's handicap value directly from the replay file for every game. The "Avg HC" column on the leaderboard shows the average handicap over a player's last 30 games:

Avg HC = sum(last 30 handicaps) ÷ 30

This gives us a rolling view of what handicap each player has been actually playing at recently, which is the baseline for recommending future handicaps.

Recommended Handicap

The goal is simple: help weaker players compete more closely with stronger ones, without over-correcting. Here's how it works:

We define a floor rating of 800 — meaning 200 points below the group average of 1000. Any player below this floor receives a handicap bump on top of what they're already using:

gap = 800 − rating
adjustment = (gap ÷ 200) × 5%
recommended = current avg HC + adjustment

The result is rounded to the nearest 5% (to match AoE2's increment) and clamped between 100% and 200%.

In plain terms: every 200 rating points below the floor earns 5% extra handicap. A few examples:

  • Rating 800, Avg HC 100% — gap is 0, adjustment is 0%. Recommended: 100% (no change).
  • Rating 700, Avg HC 100% — gap is 100, adjustment is 2.5%. Recommended: 105% (rounds up).
  • Rating 500, Avg HC 110% — gap is 300, adjustment is 7.5%. Recommended: 120% (rounds up from 117.5%).
  • Rating 1050, Avg HC 100% — above the floor, no change. Recommended: 100%.

Why Re-centering Matters for Handicaps

The recommended handicap uses the re-centered rating (with the display offset applied), not the raw TrueSkill μ. This is crucial: the 800 floor was calibrated assuming the group average is 1000. Without re-centering, as TrueSkill's average μ drifts downward, more and more players would fall below the floor and receive handicap bumps they don't actually need.

By re-centering first, the floor always means "200 points below the group average" — regardless of how much TrueSkill's internal numbers have drifted. The system stays calibrated automatically.

LAN Event Detection

The system automatically detects LAN party events from the replay data. It works by clustering games that happen close together in time:

  • Games are grouped by date. Consecutive dates with a gap of 2 days or less form a cluster.
  • A cluster is recognized as a LAN event if at least one player participated in 10 or more games during that period.
  • Detected events appear in the rating evolution chart as shaded regions, and in the Awards tab where you can filter stats per event.

Replay Uploader

Automatically upload your game replays to the War Council

⇩
Download for Windows AoE2-Uploader.zip — contains AoE2 Uploader.exe

What does it do?

The AoE2 Uploader is a lightweight Windows tool that watches your Age of Empires II replay folder and automatically uploads new .aoe2record files to the War Council server. Once uploaded, replays are parsed and player ratings, awards, and statistics are updated in real time.

No more manually copying replay files — just play and the results appear here.

Installation

  1. Download the zip file using the button above.
  2. Extract AoE2 Uploader.exe from the zip to any folder (e.g. your Desktop or Documents).
  3. Run AoE2 Uploader.exe. Windows may show a SmartScreen warning since the app is unsigned — click "More info" then "Run anyway".
  4. Configure the server URL and API key when prompted (ask the LAN party host for these).
  5. Play! The uploader runs in the background and sends new replays as soon as each game ends.

Requirements

  • Windows 10 or later
  • Age of Empires II: Definitive Edition
  • Network access to the War Council server

Troubleshooting

  • SmartScreen blocks the app — Click "More info" → "Run anyway". The exe is unsigned but safe.
  • Replays not detected — Verify the replay folder path in the uploader settings matches where AoE2:DE saves your recordings.
